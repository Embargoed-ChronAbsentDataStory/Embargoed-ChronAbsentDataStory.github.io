IN THE NATION'S SCHOOLS

An unprecedented look at a hidden educational crisis. By the U.S. Department of Education.

New Data Illuminates the Extent of Chronic Absence

A cornerstone of American education is that participation is compulsory. Compulsory education laws across the country reflect the widespread belief that young minds belong in schools—that the transfer of knowledge and the development of critical skills depend on regular exposure to teachers, peers, and rigorous and rewarding coursework.

Yet the recognition that K-12 school attendance is crucial hasn’t been coupled with a commitment to identifying and supporting the students who miss too much school: the chronically absent. Few states collect systematic information about this population. For too long, the crisis of chronic absence has been hidden from view. The consequences have been steep: inadequate supports for those impacted and weaker school-based interventions, undermined by the assumption that students are in school to receive them.

A Generation at Risk

We found that chronic absence is widespread—1 out of every 100 students missed nearly a month of school or more in 2013-14. Why did so many students fail to attend school consistently? Many students can’t, because of illness, suspension, or the need to work or take care of siblings at home; others won’t, because they feel unsafe at school or on the way to and from it; and some simply don’t, because they do not prioritize education. Poverty has been shown to exacerbate the can’t-won't-don’t dynamic.

  • Chronic absence may prevent children from reaching early learning milestones.

    Children who are chronically absent in preschool, kindergarten, and first grade are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade. Students who cannot read at grade level by the end of third grade are four times more likely than proficient readers to drop out of high school.

  • Irregular attendance can be a better predictor of high school dropout than test scores.

    A study of public school students in Utah found that an incidence of chronic absence in even a single year between eighth and twelfth grade was associated with a seven-fold increase in the likelihood of dropping out.

  • Frequent absences from school shape adulthood—usually for the worse.

    Chronic absence has been linked to worse outcomes later in life, from poverty and poorer physical and mental health to involvement in the criminal justice system.

Let’s take a closer look at chronically absent students. Disparities in chronic absence by key demographic characteristics are evident, though unacceptable levels of chronic absence exist for all types of students.

Because chronic absence is thought to be a function of structural disadvantage, the fact that chronic absenteeism mirrors wider racial inequality is unsurprising. Still, the disparities are striking.

Consider that small absolute differences in the percentage of students who are chronically absent can translate into large and disturbing relative differences. Versus their white peers, black students are 30% more likely to miss nearly a month of school or more; Hispanic students, 9% more likely. Most alarmingly, American Indian and Pacific Islander students are over 50% more likely to be chronically absent when compared to white students.

A different story emerges about the school attendance of English learners. Compared to non-English learners, they are 1.2 times less likely to be chronically absent. The finding is promising in light of America's longstanding charge to welcome and nurture immigrant children through education.

Concerning disparities appear again when we examine chronic absence by disability status. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is intended to ensure all students with disabilities have fair access to education, yet chronic absence threatens that goal: more than 17% of students with disabilities missed 15 or more days of school compared to just 12% of students without disabilities. Put another way, students with disabilities are 1.3 times more likely to be chronically absent.

The influence of gender on chronic absence is negligible. At roughly 13%, male and female students are similarly likely to miss too much school. Additional analyses revealed chronic absence was not gendered within racial or ethnic categories either.

Lost Along the Way

We've known that chronic absence is concentrated at different points along the educational pipeline. Grade levels represent distinct chapters in students' lives and reflect discrete sets of expectations, supports, and opportunities. Our mission is to ensure that, despite changing conditions along the K-12 continuum, the path toward graduation remains stable and straightforward for all students. Understanding when students are most at risk of missing too much school is the first step toward developing targeted chronic absence interventions that will help stabilize the pipeline.

Though we had to limit our analyses to the elementary-high school continuum, because of data quality concerns, data from a variety of states indicate that chronic absence is widespread in preschool and kindergarten—when it can be the most destructive. The findings here mask that issue. Still, our analyses corrobate a longstanding conclusion from the field: chronic absence thrives in high school.

The same dynamic is evident when we look at chronic absence across grade levels within groups. The disparities we saw earlier grow larger in high school, while a promising finding reverses: the stronger attendance by English learners overall disappears in high school.

The Geography of Chronic Absence

Chronic absence differs from state to state and community to community. We see chronic absence follow the contours of American poverty and disadvantage: it is higher in the low-income communities of the south and west, and you find more of it in Indian country and the urban centers of the Midwest and east. Incredibly, many school districts reported at least 30% of their students missed nearly a month of school in 2013-14.

Reaching for #EveryStudentEveryDay

Over 6 million students missed nearly a month or more of school in 2013-14. That translates to at least 93 million days of school avoided. Opportunity was lost: in a single school year, hundreds of millions of hours of classroom instruction, rich discussion, and applied work did not reach some of America’s most vulnerable students.

For the first time, the 2013-14 Civil Rights Data Collection allowed us to look more closely at the chronically absent. We found that chronic absence was highest among students of color and students with disabilities. Additionally, highly mobile and juvenile justice-involved students are particularly at risk, as identified by other research. In other words, those who already tend to face significant challenges and for whom school is most beneficial bear the worst of chronic absence.

Actionable data on the extent of chronic absence is crucial to successful reform. Once chronically absent students are identified, school systems can seize opportunities to connect them with the supports they need. Robust data empowers schools to monitor and act on a critical, underused lever of school improvement: ensuring all our students, especially the most disadvantaged, are in school every day.

Here's what the U.S. Department of Education is doing about chronic absence:

  • Every Student, Every Day: A national initiative to address and eliminate chronic absenteeism.

    Supports coordinated community action that addresses the underlying causes of local chronic absenteeism affecting millions of children in our Nation's public schools each year.

  • My Brother's Keeper: A cross-agency initiative to build ladders of opportunity for boys and young men of color.

    Data Notes

    Schools with incomplete or implausible values for the data needed for the Chronic Student Absenteeism (CSA) analyses were excluded. A total of 4,455 schools were excluded for the following reasons:

    1. The CSA question was not applicable to the school (the school must have students enrolled in grades K–12).
    2. The school reported incomplete data for enrollment and/or CSA count for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup.
    3. The CSA count was larger than the K–12 enrollment for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup. Schools with partial or missing data for students with disabilities or English learners were included in all analyses except those that focused on these subgroups.
