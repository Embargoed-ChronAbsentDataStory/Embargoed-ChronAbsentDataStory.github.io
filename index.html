New Data Illuminates the Extent of Chronic Absenteeism

Education can only fulfill its promise as the great equalizer —a force that can overcome differences in privilege and background— when we work to ensure that students are in school every day and receive the supports they need to learn and thrive.

At the same time, we know that many students experience tremendous adversity in their lives—including poverty, health challenges, community violence, and difficult family circumstances—that can make it difficult for them to get to school every day to take advantage of opportunities to learn and grow.

Students who are chronically absent—meaning they miss at least 15 days of school in a year—are at serious risk of falling behind in school. Yet few states collect information about chronic absenteeism, and for too long this crisis in our nation’s public elementary and secondary schools has not been fully understood. The release of the U.S. Department’s 2013-2014 Civil Rights Data Collection marks a turning point in the effort to reduce and ultimately eliminate chronic absenteeism so that all students can reach their full potential. For the first time, the CRDC collected data on students who miss at least three weeks of school. 99.5% of all public schools participated in the survey, resulting in the most comprehensive portrait of chronic absenteeism in our country to date.

This interactive data story analyzes the new data to explore who is most impacted by chronic absenteeism; when in the educational pipeline it tends to be severe; and how chronic absenteeism compares in cities and towns across the country.

OVER 6 MILLION students missed 15 or more days of school in 2013-2014.

That's 13% of the student population—or almost 1 out of every 8 students.

A Generation at Risk

The Department of Education found that chronic absenteeism is widespread—almost one out of every eight students missed three weeks or more of school in 2013-2014. Why were so many students absent from school? The reasons are as varied as the challenges our students and families face—including health, transportation, and safety—which can be particularly acute in disadvantaged communities and areas of concentrated poverty.

Whatever its causes, chronic absenteeism can be devastating:

  • Chronic absenteeism may prevent children from reaching early learning milestones.

    Children who are chronically absent in preschool, kindergarten, and first grade are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade. Students who cannot read at grade level by the end of third grade are four times more likely than proficient readers to drop out of high school.

  • Irregular attendance can be a better predictor of high school dropout than test scores.

    A study of public school students in Utah found that an incidence of chronic absenteeism in even a single year between eighth and 12th grade was associated with a seven-fold increase in the likelihood of dropping out.

  • Frequent absences from school can shape adulthood.

    Chronic absenteeism has been linked to poor outcomes later in life, from poverty and diminished physical and mental health to involvement in the criminal justice system.

Let’s take a closer look at which groups of students are more likely to be chronically absent. Disparities in chronic absenteeism by key demographic characteristics are evident, though unacceptable levels of chronic absenteeism exist for all students.

The disparities are striking. Consider the relative differences: compared to their white peers, black students are 30 percent more likely to lose three weeks of school or more in a year, Hispanic students 9 percent more likely, and American Indian and Pacific Islander students over 50 percent more likely.

There is a bright spot in the data: English learners are 1.2 times less likely to be chronically absent then their non-English learner peers. Less than 11 percent of English learners are chronically absent compared to more than 13 percent of non-English learners.

The same is not true for students with disabilities who are 1.3 times more likely to be chronically absent than students without disabilities. The federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is intended to ensure all students with disabilities have access to a free, appropriate education yet chronic absence complicates efforts toward that goal.

At roughly 13 percent each, male and female students are similarly likely to be chronically absent. Additional analyses revealed this symmetry held true within racial and ethnic categories as well.

Lost Along the Way

Chronic absence occurs at every grade level but is more concentrated in some grades than others. Understanding when students are most at risk of missing too much school along the K-12 continuum is the first step toward developing targeted interventions for chronic absenteeism that will help strengthen the entire public education pipeline. CRDC contains data at the school level and shows that, overall, high schools have the highest rates of chronic absenteeism. However, other research suggests that students in the early elementary grades also experience high rates of chronic absenteeism, which may be masked when combined with the other elementary grades.

Nearly 1 in 5 students in high school are chronically absent.

For each subgroup of students there is a similar pattern: the likelihood of chronic absenteeism increases as students progress though middle and high school. Notably, the stronger attendance observed among English learners overall dissipates over time, such that they experience higher chronic absenteeism than their non-English learner peers when they reach high school.

The Path Forward: Every Student Every Day

The analyses here support the idea that the very students who tend to face significant challenges and need the most educational supports are often those most impacted by chronic absenteeism.

When our teachers, principals, policymakers, and others have access to robust data on the extent and nature of chronic absenteeism, all of us are in a better position to provide students with the supports they need to stem this crisis in our schools.

Data, like the information presented here, can empower schools to monitor and act on a critical, underused lever of education improvement: ensuring all our students, especially the most disadvantaged, are in school every day.

The U.S. Department of Education is committed to helping address chronic absenteeism through a variety of initiatives:

  • Every Student, Every Day: A national initiative to address and eliminate chronic absenteeism.

    Supports coordinated community action that addresses the underlying causes of local chronic absenteeism affecting millions of children in our Nation's public schools each year.

  • My Brother's Keeper: A cross-agency initiative to build ladders of opportunity for boys and young men of color.

    Text.

  • The Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC): A biennial survey required by the U.S. Department of Education’s Office for Civil Rights since 1968.

    The CRDC shines a spotlight on data that enable us to understand the successes and challenges of schools and school districts in providing educational opportunity to all of our nation’s children.
