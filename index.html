CHRONIC ABSENCE
IN THE NATION'S SCHOOLS

An unprecedented look at a hidden educational crisis. By the U.S. Department of Education.

  • Continue

New Data Illuminates the Extent of Chronic Absence

A cornerstone of American education is that participation is compulsory. Compulsory education laws across the country reflect the widespread belief that young minds belong in schools—that the transfer of knowledge and the development of critical skills depend on regular exposure to teachers, peers, and rigorous and rewarding coursework.

Yet the recognition that K-12 school attendance is crucial hasn’t been coupled with a commitment to identifying and supporting the students who miss too much school: the chronically absent. Few states collect systematic information about this population. For too long, the crisis of chronic absence has been hidden from view. The consequences have been steep: inadequate supports for those impacted and weaker school-based interventions, undermined by the assumption that students are in school to receive them.

That blindspot shrunk considerably with Tuesday’s release of the 2013-14 Civil Rights Data Collection. To compile this data collection, the U.S. Department of Education’s Office for Civil Rights asked U.S. public schools to report the number of students who missed 15 or more days of school in 2013-14. 99.7% of public schools responded, offering the most comprehensive portrait of chronic absence in the nation to date.

In this interactive data story, we explore who is most impacted by chronic absence; when in the educational pipeline it tends to be severe; and how it compares across the country.

A Generation at Risk

We found that chronic absence is widespread—1 out of every 100 students missed nearly a month of school or more in 2013-14. Why did so many students fail to attend school consistently? Many students can’t, because of illness, suspension, or the need to work or take care of siblings at home; others won’t, because they feel unsafe at school or on the way to and from it; and some simply don’t, because they do not prioritize education. Poverty has been shown to exacerbate the can’t-won't-don’t dynamic.

Whatever its basis, chronic absence can be devastating:

  • Chronic absence may prevent children from reaching early learning milestones.

    Children who are chronically absent in preschool, kindergarten, and first grade are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade. Students who cannot read at grade level by the end of third grade are four times more likely than proficient readers to drop out of high school.

  • Irregular attendance can be a better predictor of high school dropout than test scores.

    A study of public school students in Utah found that an incidence of chronic absence in even a single year between eighth and twelfth grade was associated with a seven-fold increase in the likelihood of dropping out.

  • Frequent absences from school shape adulthood—usually for the worse.

    Chronic absence has been linked to worse outcomes later in life, from poverty and poorer physical and mental health to involvement in the criminal justice system.

Let’s take a closer look at chronically absent students. Disparities in chronic absence by key student characteristics are evident, though unacceptable levels of chronic absence exist for all types of students.

That chronic absenteeism reflects wider racial inequality is unsurprising; chronic absence is thought to be a function of structural disadvantage. Still, the disparities are striking.

Consider that small absolute differences in the percentage of students who are chronically absent can translate into large and disturbing relative differences. Versus their white peers, black students are 30% more likely to be chronically absent; Hispanic students, 9% more likely. Most alarmingly, American Indian and Pacific Islander students are over 50% more likely to miss too much school when compared to white students.

A different story emerges about the school attendance of English learners. Compared to non-English learners, they are 1.2 times less likely to be chronically absent. The finding is promising in light of America's longstanding charge to welcome and nurture immigrant children through education.

Concerning disparities appear again when we examine chronic absence by disability status. The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) is intended to ensure all students with disabilities have fair access to education, yet chronic absence threatens that goal: more than 17% of students with disabilities are chronically absent compared to just 12% of students without disabilities. Put another way, students with disabilities are 1.3 times more likely to miss 15 or more days of school.

The influence of gender on chronic absence is negligible. At roughly 13%, male and female students are similarly likely to miss 15 or more days of school. Additional analyses revealed chronic absence was not gendered within racial or ethnic categories either.

Chronic Absenteeism in the Pipeline

We know that chronic absence is concentrated at different points along the educational pipeline. Though we had to limit our analyses to the elementary-high school continuum, because of data quality concerns, data from a variety of states indicate that chronic absence is widespread in preschool and kindergarten—when it can be the most destructive.
