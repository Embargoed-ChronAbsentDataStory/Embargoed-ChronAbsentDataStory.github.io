New Data Illuminates the Extent of Chronic Absenteeism

Education can only fulfill its promise as the great equalizer —a force that can overcome differences in privilege and background— when we work to ensure that students are in school every day and receive the supports they need to learn and thrive.

At the same time, we know that many students experience tremendous adversity in their lives—including poverty, health challenges, community violence, and difficult family circumstances—that can make it difficult for them to get to school every day to take advantage of opportunities to learn and grow.

Students who are chronically absent—meaning they miss at least 15 days of school in a year—are at serious risk of falling behind in school. Yet few states collect information about chronic absenteeism, and for too long this crisis in our nation’s public elementary and secondary schools has not been fully understood. The release of the U.S. Department’s 2013-2014 Civil Rights Data Collection marks a turning point in the effort to reduce and ultimately eliminate chronic absenteeism so that all students can reach their full potential. For the first time, the CRDC collected data on students who miss at least three weeks of school. 99.5% of all public schools participated in the survey, resulting in the most comprehensive portrait of chronic absenteeism in our country to date.

Using the new data, this interactive data story explores who is most impacted by chronic absenteeism; when in the educational pipeline it tends to be severe; and how chronic absenteeism compares in cities and towns across the country.

OVER 6 MILLION students missed 15 or more days of school in 2013-2014.

That's 13% of the student population—or almost 1 out of every 8 students.

A Generation at Risk

The Department of Education found that chronic absenteeism is widespread—almost one out of every eight students missed three weeks or more of school in 2013-2014. Why were so many students absent from school? The reasons are as varied as the challenges our students and families face—including health, transportation, and safety—which can be particularly acute in disadvantaged communities and areas of concentrated poverty.

Whatever its causes, chronic absenteeism can be devastating:

  • Chronic absenteeism may prevent children from reaching early learning milestones.

    Children who are chronically absent in preschool, kindergarten, and first grade are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade. Students who cannot read at grade level by the end of third grade are four times more likely than proficient readers to drop out of high school.

  • Irregular attendance can be a better predictor of high school dropout than test scores.

    A study of public school students in Utah found that an incidence of chronic absenteeism in even a single year between eighth and 12th grade was associated with a seven-fold increase in the likelihood of dropping out.

  • Frequent absences from school can shape adulthood.

    Chronic absenteeism has been linked to poor outcomes later in life, from poverty and diminished physical and mental health to involvement in the criminal justice system.

Let’s take a closer look at which groups of students are more likely to be chronically absent. Disparities in chronic absenteeism by key demographic characteristics are evident, though unacceptable levels of chronic absenteeism exist for all students.

The disparities are striking. Consider the relative differences: compared to their white peers, black students are 30 percent more likely to lose three weeks of school or more in a year, Hispanic students are 9 percent more likely, and American Indian and Pacific Islander students are over 50 percent more likely to be chronically absent.

A bright spot in the data - English learners are 1.2 times less likely to be chronically absent that their non-English learner peers. Less than 11% of English learners were chronically absent compared to more than 13% of non-English learners.

The same is not true for students with disabilities who are 1.3 times more likely to be chronically absent than students without disabilities. More than 17% of students with disabilities missed 15 or more days of school compared to just 12% of students without disabilities.

At roughly 13%, male and female students are similarly likely to be chronically absent. Additional analyses revealed this symmetry held true within racial and ethnic categories as well.

Lost Along the Way

Research suggests that chronic absenteeism is not evenly distributed across all grades and is instead concentrated at specific points along the educational pipeline, namely the early grades and the later years of high school. Understanding when students are most at risk of missing too much school along the K-12 continuum is the first step toward developing targeted chronic absenteeism interventions that will help stabilize the pipeline.

CRDC data collects data at the school level (elementary, middle, high school) so the prevalence of chronic absenteeism in specific grades is masked. When looking broadly across the school levels, chronic absenteeism is highest in high school.

Overall, 19% of students in high school are chronically absent compared to 12% of students in middle school. Students in elementary schools had the lowest chronic absenteeism rate, 10%, though this may be masking high rates of chronic absenteeism in the early elementary grades.

For each subgroup of students chronic absenteeism follows a similar pattern with the likelihood of students being chronically absent increasing as students progress though middle and high school. Notably, the lower rates of chronic absenteeism that is seen when examining English learners overall dissipates when these students reach high school.
