New Data Illuminates the Extent of Chronic Absence

Young minds belong in schools. The transfer of knowledge and the development of critical skills depend on regular exposure to teachers, peers, and rigorous and rewarding coursework.

Missing school frequently has negative consequences on students. For example:

  • Students who are chronically absent in early grades are much less likely to read at grade level by the third grade.
  • Irregular attendance can be a better predictor of high school dropout than test scores.
  • Chronic absenteeism has been linked to worse outcomes later in life, including poverty, poorer physical and mental health, and involvement in the criminal justice system.

For too long, the crisis of chronic absenteeism has been hidden from view. This blindspot shrunk considerably with the 2013-14 Civil Rights Data Collection (CRDC), which for the first time, collects information on the number and characteristics of students who missed 15 or more days of school in the 2013-14 school year. The CRDC includes data from 99.5% of all public schools and this collection offers the most comprehensive national portrait of chronic absence to date.

    Data Note

    Schools with incomplete or implausible values for the data needed for the Chronic Student Absenteeism (CSA) analyses were excluded. A total of 4,455 schools were excluded for the following reasons:

    1. The CSA question was not applicable to the school (the school must have students enrolled in grades K–12).
    2. The school reported incomplete data for enrollment and/or CSA count for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup.
    3. The CSA count was larger than the K–12 enrollment for at least one race/ethnicity subgroup. Schools with partial or missing data for students with disabilities or English learners were included in all analyses except those that focused on these subgroups.

OVER
6 MILLION students missed 15 or more days of school in 2013-14.

That's 13%—or 1 out of every 8—of all students.

The disparities are striking – compared to white students, black students are 30% more likely to miss nearly a month of school or more; Hispanic students are 9% more likely and American Indian and Pacific Islander students are over 50% more likely to be chronically absent. Hover over each bar to see the exact figures for how many students in each group were chronically absent.

A bright spot in the data - English learners are 1.2 times less likely to be chronically absent that their non-English learner peers. Less than 11% of English learners were chronically absent compared to more than 13% of non-English learners.

The same is not true for students with disabilities who are 1.3 times more likely to be chronically absent than students without disabilities. More than 17% of students with disabilities missed 15 or more days of school compared to just 12% of students without disabilities.

At roughly 13%, male and female students are similarly likely to be chronically absent. Additional analyses revealed this symmetry held true within racial and ethnic categories as well.

Lost Along the Way

Research suggests that chronic absence is not evenly distributed across all grades and is instead concentrated at specific points along the educational pipeline, namely the early grades and the later years of high school. Understanding when students are most at risk of missing too much school along the K-12 continuum is the first step toward developing targeted chronic absence interventions that will help stabilize the pipeline.

CRDC data collects data at the school level (elementary, middle, high school) so the prevalence of chronic absenteeism in specific grades is masked. When looking broadly across the school levels, chronic absenteeism is highest in high school.

Overall, 19% of students in high school are chronically absent compared to 12% of students in middle school. Students in elementary schools had the lowest chronic absenteeism rate, 10%, though this may be masking high rates of chronic absenteeism in the early elementary grades.

For each subgroup of students chronic absence follows a similar pattern with the likelihood of students being chronically absent increasing as students progress though middle and high school. Notably, the lower rates of chronic absenteeism that is seen when examining English learners overall dissipates when these students reach high school.
